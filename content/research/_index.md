      <h3>What Prediction Markets and Options Know About Earnings Expectations</h3>
      <p class="paper-meta">Solo-authored</p>
      <p><span class="paper-label">Abstract:</span> [Recent work shows that financial prediction markets measure the direction of earnings surprises, while option-implied volatility already
measures the other moment, the magnitude of the move. I show the two are not
competing forecasts but complementary halves of one, and that using either
alone is systematically wrong. Matching Polymarket ``beat-the-consensus''
earnings contracts to Refinitiv option series, I confirm the division of
labour in the announcement return itself: the prediction-market surprise
prices the return's sign and is incremental to the analyst surprise, while
pre-announcement implied volatility prices its size. The cost of using one
alone is concrete: an at-the-money option, symmetric by construction, assigns
the same distribution to a firm about to beat and one about to miss---halving
the assessed probability of a large drop for those about to disappoint---while
a prediction market, lacking a scale, under-covers its risk bands for volatile
names. Only together do the two isolate the extreme returns---a likely miss
with a large implied move averages $-3.6\%$, against $+1.9\%$ for a likely
beat---and price the tail correctly. Prediction markets and options are
therefore complements rather than substitutes: earnings risk measured from
either one alone is not merely imprecise, but systematically biased.]</p>
